Fashion

Explore men's, women's, and children's fashion trends during the 1920s.

Source: Fashion History Timeline

"La Garçonne"

The La Garçonne look, also known as the Flapper, was one of the most popular styles of dress for women in the 1920s. The look consisted of a drop waistline, loose fit, and detailed beading, made of economical fabric.

Photo: Alice Joyce in a straight dress

Tennis + Sportswear

Throughout the 1920s, the sport of tennis grew in popularity. With it, so did tennis attire. It became common for both men and women.

Photo: Women's Tennis

Plus Fours

Plus Fours were a popular type of mens trouser. The name originated from the four extra inches of fabric that gathered around the knee. Starting as clothing for golf, the made there way into everyday fashion.

Photo: William Orpen - Edward, Prince of Wales

Children's Fashion

1920s children's fashion prioritized clothes that were comfortable and more economical. Clothes were more loose fitting so that children were able to play. In addition, they became more simple, so much so it was common to purchase patterns and make styles at home.

Photo: Roller-skater, Mississippi

Radio

In the 1920s, the radio was by far the most popular form of music consumption. Everything from news, speeches, the weather, and sports was broadcast on the radio. It was a source of both communication and entertainment.

Photo: Movie star listening to radio

Jazz

One of the most popular styles of music in the 1920s was Jazz. Jazz music was so popular in this decade that it is often referred to as the Jazz Age. The music style started in New Orleans, Louisiana and was a combination of Anglo-American, African, and Creole influences.

Photo: King & Carter Jazzing Orchestra

Blues

Another popular style of music in the 20s was Blues. The genre was inspired by African sprituals. Many of the songs were about the prejudice and segration in the South.

Photo: Cannon's Jug Stompers

The Charleston

The Charleston was the most popular form of dance music. It became popular along with the same style of dance by the same name. Performed by big band orchestars, it was common in dance halls across the US.

Photo: The Charleston

Art

All about the art movements in the 1920s. Learn about Art Deco, Surrealism, Expressionism, and Dada.

Source: Art Movements of the 1920s

Art Deco

Art Deco was the most popular style of art in the 1920s. It emerged between WW1 and WW2. It combined traditional media and the technology at the time.

Photo: quintessential Art Deco building

Expressionism

Expressionism was another popular art style in the 1920s. It was known for its distorted feel. The art intented to evoke emotion, moods, and ides, being less focused on reality.

Photo: The Scream

Dada

Starting in WWI, Dada was an art movement that was know for it political commentary. It went against the political conformity at the time. As a result, it features chaotic and irritionality in its concepts.

Photo: Cut with the Kitchen Knife

Surrelism

Also stemming from WW1, Surrelism was a similar popular art style. It also was nknow for its social and political commentary. This was expressed through its suprising and unexpected compositions.

Photo: The Elephant Celebes

Movies

Movies grew drastically in popularity during the 1920s. Theaters became much larger, with some cities having theaters that could hold thousands. By the end of the decade the first "talkie" was released, beginning the end of silent films.

Photo: Chaplin and Edna Purviance

Automobiles

The automobile was become increasingly popular during this decade. With the advancement of technology, cars were now able to be mass produced. This led to them becoming more affordable and widely accessible.

Photo: Model A

Sports

Sports were a very popular form of entertainment, even in the 1920s. Baseball and tennis were the most popular at the time. With the popularity of the radio, sports could even be enjoyed from the comfort of home!

Photo: Tennis Player
